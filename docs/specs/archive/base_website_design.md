The site should feel like a professional analysis product rather than a generic dashboard. The tone should be clean, deliberate, and information-dense without feeling cramped. A neutral visual style with strong typography, restrained color, and clear visual hierarchy will make the work feel serious. The goal is to make a potential employer or peer think, “this person understands how analysts actually explore data.”

The landing page should immediately answer three questions: what this site contains, why it matters, and what the visitor can do first. The top section should introduce the labor-market occupation project in a few sentences, then present a few high-value entry points such as exploring occupations, comparing geographies, reviewing skill profiles, and viewing methodology. It should feel like an analytical product with a guided starting point, not just a pile of charts.

The overall layout should revolve around a persistent top navigation and a flexible content area. The navigation should expose a small set of major sections: Overview, Occupations, Geography, Skills, Trends, Methodology, and About This Project. A left-side contextual panel can appear on deeper pages to expose filters and comparison controls, but the main experience should stay focused on the data, not on chrome.

The visual style should emphasize readability. Headings should be strong and compact. Body text should be concise and useful. Cards, tables, charts, and filters should all align to a consistent spacing system. Important numbers should stand out, but the page should avoid bright consumer-style colors or decorative graphics. The site should look closer to a research platform or executive analytics product than to a marketing page.

The most important page is probably the occupation profile page. This page should act as the center of the experience. At the top, it should show the occupation name, formal code, hierarchy placement, and a short definition. Immediately below that, it should present key summary panels for employment, wage range, projected outlook if included, and a compact skill or task snapshot. The rest of the page should move downward from summary to depth: geographic distribution, wage distribution, related occupations, skill profile, task profile, and any notes on source lineage or interpretation. A visitor should be able to understand the occupation in thirty seconds, then keep drilling deeper without changing mental context.

A geography page should be built around comparison. It should let the visitor choose a geography and quickly see which occupations are largest, which pay best, and which differ most from national norms. It should support side-by-side comparison between states or metro areas, with changes clearly visible and not buried in chart menus. The feel should be exploratory but controlled.

A skills page should present labor data from the semantic angle. Instead of leading with occupations, it should lead with descriptors such as skills, knowledge areas, or tasks, then show which occupations are strongly associated with them. This helps the site stand out because it demonstrates that the project is more than a wage table browser. It shows a semantic model in action.

A trends page should focus on time and comparability. It should make clear when the user is looking at as-published history versus comparable trend history. That distinction is one of the strongest signals of rigor in the whole project, so the interface should make it visible instead of hiding it in documentation. If the data includes projections, this page should also show the difference between historical observations and forward-looking estimates.

A methodology page should be a first-class part of the product, not an afterthought. It should explain the sources, the normalization approach, data versioning, validation logic, known limitations, and the difference between occupation, title, and semantic descriptors. This page helps demonstrate judgment and discipline. It also gives the site credibility with more technical reviewers.

The core interactive behavior should be based on guided filtering, comparison, and drill-down. Users should be able to search occupations quickly, filter by major group, geography, wage band, or semantic attributes, and save the current context visually as they move between pages. Every page should preserve the user’s sense of place. The site should not feel like each click resets the analysis.

Tables should be treated as serious analytical tools. They should support sorting, searching, column selection, and export-friendly views. Charts should support hover details and click-through into a deeper view, but should not become overly animated or decorative. A dense, well-designed table will often communicate credibility better than a flashy chart.

The site should also include a small “portfolio lens.” This does not mean self-promotion all over the interface. It means there should be a subtle but clear way for a reviewer to understand what they are seeing: what parts show data modeling, what parts show semantic enrichment, what parts show data quality methodology, and what parts show analytical thinking. A small section such as “What this demonstrates” on the overview or methodology page would do that well.

From a feature perspective, the strongest top-level set is: fast occupation search, hierarchy browsing, geography comparison, skill and task exploration, trend views, source/version notes, downloadable tables, and guided methodology pages. That is enough to make the site feel complete without turning it into a giant platform.

The look and feel should communicate three things at once: analytical seriousness, usability, and taste. The functionality should center on exploring occupations from multiple angles. The features should quietly demonstrate your strengths in modeling, analysis, and tool thinking. If the site does that, it becomes more than a report viewer. It becomes evidence of how you think.
